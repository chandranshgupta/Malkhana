Compliance and Operational Framework for the Indian Digital Malkhana: Securing the Electronic Chain-of-Custody under BNS, BSA, BNSS, and the DPDP Act 2023Section 1: Statutory Transition and the New Legal Regime of Digital EvidenceThe Indian criminal justice system has undergone a structural transformation with the repeal of colonial-era laws and the enactment of the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023 (BSA), which replaces the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 (IEA) ; the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS), which supersedes the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC) ; and the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 (BNS), which replaces the Indian Penal Code, 1860 (IPC). This statutory transition elevates electronic records from a secondary, conditional category of proof to mainstream documentary evidence. Section 2 of the BSA expands the definitions of both "document" and "evidence" to explicitly encompass digital outputs, including emails, server logs, smartphone records, locational coordinates, and voice mails. Section 57 of the BSA establishes that electronic records generated under proper custody are recognized as documentary evidence on par with physical paper documents, eliminating historical ambiguities regarding their core admissibility.                ┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐

Admissibility is governed by the specialized mechanisms under Sections 61, 62, and 63 of the BSA. Section 61 serves as the foundational provision, mandating that the contents of electronic records must be proved in accordance with Section 63. Under the BSA, a clear distinction is drawn between primary and secondary electronic evidence :Primary Electronic Evidence: The original electronic source or device itself (e.g., the physical smartphone, hard drive, or live server in its native format). Where a video or digital record is simultaneously stored and transmitted or broadcast, each stored recording constitutes primary evidence.Secondary Electronic Evidence: Any computer output, printout, screenshot, or digital copy extracted from the primary source. These secondary forms strictly require authentication and certification to be admissible.Under Section 63(4) of the BSA, any secondary electronic record must be accompanied by a mandatory certificate at each instance of its submission for admission. This statutory certificate is divided into a dual-part framework:Part A (The Custodian Statement): Completed by the person occupying a "responsible official position" who has lawful control over the device or system that generated, stored, or processed the target data. This party must affirm the device's technical details (Make, Model, Serial Number, IMEI, MAC Address, or Cloud ID) and testify that the system was operating properly in the ordinary course of business.Part B (The Expert Validation): Completed by a qualified digital forensics expert recognized under Section 39 of the BSA, confirming the cryptographic hash value (such as SHA-256 or MD5) of the extracted digital record to certify its scientific integrity.Section 62 introduces a narrow judicial safety valve, allowing courts to waive the certificate requirement under exceptional circumstances where obtaining it is proved to be "not reasonably practicable" (such as when data resides on defunct systems or foreign-hosted platforms that refuse cooperation). Additionally, Section 63(1) establishes a rebuttable presumption of integrity for secure electronic records that bear a valid digital signature or are issued as government certificates.This evidentiary regime is procedurally enforced by the BNSS. Under BNSS Section 105, any search and seizure operation conducted by law enforcement must be documented via mandatory audio-video recording, and this media must be submitted before a competent Magistrate "without delay" to minimize the opportunity for evidence tampering. Furthermore, BNSS Section 176(3) mandates that for all offences carrying a statutory punishment of seven or more years of imprisonment, a forensic expert must visit the crime scene to collect physical and digital evidence, and the entire extraction process must be videographed. Additionally, under BNSS Section 173, the police must display guidelines for the public regarding access to investigations , and BNSS Section 530 provides legal sanction for the electronic service of summons, notices, and warrants. Where an accused in custody is examined electronically through video conferencing, his signature on the recorded statement must be obtained within 72 hours.To bridge the gap between field investigations and court trials, Section 39(2) of the BSA recognizes the expert opinions of the "Examiner of Electronic Evidence" as designated under Section 79A of the IT Act, 2000. The Examiner of Electronic Evidence is a specialized government agency or laboratory formally notified by MeitY after a comprehensive technical audit of its forensic competence. When a Digital Malkhana ingests forensic images, these must be evaluated, signed, and certified by these recognized entities to satisfy the legal tests of reliability during judicial scrutiny.Section 2: Indian Cyber Regulations and Data Protection HarmonizationThe operationalization of a Digital Malkhana in India requires reconciling overlapping legislative and regulatory frameworks. Enforcement Exemptions with Data SafeguardsThe Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) Act, 2023 establishes strict rules for corporate entities regarding consent, purpose limitation, and the mandatory erasure of personal data once the specified purpose of its processing is no longer served. However, Section 17 of the DPDP Act provides critical exemptions for law enforcement, judicial, and state security apparatuses. Section 17(1)(b) exempts the processing of personal data by any court, tribunal, or quasi-judicial body in India from most obligations of the Act. Section 17(1)(c) permits the processing of personal data without consent or erasure constraints if it is done in the interest of prevention, detection, investigation, or prosecution of any offence or contravention of any law. Furthermore, Section 17(2)(a) allows the Central Government to completely exempt specific State instrumentalities in the interest of national sovereignty, state security, or public order.Despite these extensive exemptions, Section 17(1) explicitly preserves the application of Section 8(5) of the DPDP Act. This means that even when processing digital evidence exempt from consent and erasure rules, the state and its instrumentalities must implement reasonable security safeguards to prevent personal data breaches. A failure to secure the Digital Malkhana from unauthorized access, leakage, or tampering remains a direct violation of the DPDP Act, attracting severe penalties and violating the constitutional right to informational privacy under Article 21.Preserving Log Integrity under CERT-In and MeitY GuidelinesUnder Section 70B of the IT Act, CERT-In's cybersecurity directions mandate that all government agencies, intermediaries, and body corporates must securely maintain all ICT system logs for a rolling period of 180 days within Indian jurisdiction. These logs must be provided to CERT-In upon request during any cyber incident response. Furthermore, all system clocks must connect to the Network Time Protocol (NTP) servers of the National Informatics Centre (NIC) (samay1.nic.in, samay2.nic.in) or the National Physical Laboratory (NPL) (time.nplindia.org) to guarantee uniform time synchronization across the digital ecosystem.Concurrently, MeitY guidelines restrict hosting to MeitY-empaneled secure cloud environments (such as the GI Cloud "MeghRaj"), ensuring strict data localization within India's borders. These cloud service providers (CSPs) undergo regular compliance audits by the Standardisation Testing and Quality Certification (STQC) Directorate to verify adherence to ISO/IEC 27001 (Information Security Management), ISO/IEC 27017 (Cloud Security), and ISO/IEC 27018 (Cloud Privacy). The system architecture is structured into four secured tiers:1. Application Layer (OWASP ASVS Level 3 Compliance)The application portal and API gateways must conform to the Advanced Verification requirements of the OWASP Application Security Verification Standard (ASVS) Level 3. This high-assurance standard is mandatory for governmental, defense, and high-risk transactional environments where security failure presents severe systemic risks. Key application controls include:Authentication & Session Management: Multi-factor authentication (MFA) utilizing hardware tokens or FIDO2 keys is enforced for all system logins. Session identifiers must be generated exclusively by the underlying secure framework and configured with absolute timeouts.Input Validation & Encoding: Contextual output encoding and parameterized queries are utilized to eliminate command injection, SQL injection, and cross-site scripting (XSS) risks.Server-Side Access Control: Explicit, role-based access control (RBAC) is validated directly on the server side. Access control decisions and failed access attempts must trigger immediate, high-priority alerts logged directly to a secure, write-once log server.Web Frontend Security: Auto-escaping technology is applied to all HTML sanitization routines, and browser memory must be cleared of sensitive data upon session termination.2. Forensic Ingestion LayerThis layer manages the extraction, hashing, and storage of ingested digital records.WORM (Write Once Read Many) Storage: All primary and secondary digital evidence must be committed to physical or logical WORM storage drives. Once a file is ingested, the system architecture must physically prevent any alteration, overwrite, or deletion of the target block.Hardware Cryptographic Engine: Hash values must be calculated using dedicated Hardware Security Modules (HSM) implementing the SHA-256 algorithm :$$H(M) \rightarrow \{0, 1\}^{256}$$where $M$ represents the raw binary stream of the ingested digital file, ensuring that any single-bit alteration of the evidence ($M \neq M'$) results in a completely non-colliding hash output ($H(M) \neq H(M')$).NTP Synchronization Engine: To maintain absolute temporal consistency, the database servers must run a resilient local NTP daemon configured to synchronize with Stratum-1 time servers traceable directly to the National Physical Laboratory (NPL) and NIC NTP servers :samay1.nic.in samay2.nic.in time.nplindia.org 
This synchronization must execute continuously, and any drift exceeding $10 \text{ milliseconds}$ must generate a system-level audit exception.3. Unified CCTNS and ICJS IntegrationThe Digital Malkhana must not function as an isolated database; it must integrate with CCTNS and the national ICJS database. Data flow is orchestrated using the e-Committee of the Supreme Court of India's approved Data Sharing Matrix :API Ingestion: The platform consumes FIR and chargesheet data from CCTNS, mapping central and state acts to National Codes. Interconnection requires firewall clearance for ports 80 and 443 between the local repository servers and the central ICJS server.Automated Forensics Alignment: Case property must be mapped to CCTNS using automated data capture. Once a forensic expert uploads a digital report or the court enters a judgment, the e-Forensics and e-Prosecution applications automatically synchronize key metadata fields (such as case disposal status) to trigger the appropriate retention or disposal workflows in the repository.4. Physical Storage and Environmental SecurityFor physical storage rooms housing seized electronic devices (such as laptops, smartphones, DVRs, or external storage drives) before forensic imaging, strict physical and climate controls must be implemented :Faraday Isolation: All mobile and wireless-enabled devices must be powered down or placed inside certified Faraday bags immediately upon seizure to block all RF signals, preventing remote data wiping, cellular triangulation, or over-the-air updates.Particulate and Climate Control: Vaults must maintain dust-free, ESD-safe conditions, with climate control preserving an ambient temperature of $18^\circ\text{C} \text{ to } 22^\circ\text{C}$ and relative humidity between $40\% \text{ and } 50\%$ to prevent logical component degradation. High-intensity magnetic fields must be strictly isolated to prevent media corruption.Surveillance & Perimeter Security: The facility must implement biometric access control, motion-activated CCTV recording committed to a dedicated secure storage node, and physical dual-custody access locks.Section 4: Forensic Chain-of-Custody and Evidence Lifecycle GovernanceTo ensure that electronic evidence survives judicial scrutiny under Section 63 of the BSA, the Digital Malkhana must enforce a standardized operational lifecycle. The evidence lifecycle consists of the following phases:1. Identification (ISO/IEC 27037)This phase establishes which devices and digital data sources contain potential digital evidence of value. First responders must document the initial state of the device (powered on or off) and capture surrounding metadata.Powered-On Systems: Require volatile data capture—including active network connections, active system RAM contents, and unencrypted file structures—before powering down.Powered-Off Systems: Must not be booted or powered on under any circumstances, to prevent metadata alteration.2. Collection (ISO/IEC 27037)This phase involves the physical removal of the storage media or device from the scene to a controlled lab or Digital Malkhana environment. Investigators must log the transport details, seal physical media using tamper-evident evidence tags, and register barcodes into the Malkhana tracking software.3. Acquisition (ISO/IEC 27037)This phase involves obtaining a digital copy of the target device without compromising its logical integrity.Investigators must generate a bit-for-bit raw copy of the physical storage media (forensic imaging) using hardware write-blockers that prevent any data write-back to the source drive.Immediately post-acquisition, cryptographic hashing must run over the source drive and the destination image. A complete hash match confirms that the copy is identical, satisfying the core principles of digital forensics.4. Preservation (ISO/IEC 27037)The digital image must be committed to the secure Digital Malkhana repository with an unbroken, digital chain-of-custody log. In line with ISO/IEC 27037, every single transaction, access request, and administrative action performed on the digital file must be recorded in real time.This operational framework must maintain conformity with the four fundamental quality principles of digital evidence :Auditability: Every administrative action, system event, and access request must be recorded in an unalterable log, allowing an independent auditor to reconstruct the entire sequence of events.Repeatability: Under the same environment and tools, executing the same acquisition or extraction process on the original evidence must yield identical results.Reproducibility: An independent third-party investigator using different tools, platforms, or analytical methods must be able to reproduce the same findings and verify the matching hash values.Justifiability: Any operational action taken that alters the system state (such as conducting a live acquisition on active RAM, which inevitably alters temporary logs) must be technically justified, documented, and performed by a qualified specialist.To ensure the records remain authoritative and defensible over decades, the Digital Malkhana integrates the records management principles of ISO 15489. In line with this standard, the system must guarantee that all stored digital evidence exhibits four key characteristics :Authenticity: The record can be proven to be what it purports to be, having been created or sent by the verified person at the documented time.Reliability: The contents accurately represent the target transaction, incident, or physical state without distortion.Integrity: The record is complete and unaltered by any subsequent unauthorized operations.Usability: The digital evidence can be easily located, retrieved, presented, and interpreted by authorized judicial actors throughout the trial process.To support these principles, the repository must implement the structural processes of ISO/IEC 27041 (suitability and validation of forensic methods to ensure that tools do not introduce errors) , ISO/IEC 27042 (methodological framework for the analysis and interpretation of digital evidence using static, dynamic, or real-time forensics) , and ISO/IEC 27043 (overarching incident investigation processes, including scene documentation, evidence transit, and final trial presentation).These guidelines are further aligned with the United States NIST SP 800-86 (Guide to Integrating Forensic Techniques into Incident Response), which defines a structured approach for data collection, examination, analysis, and reporting , and INTERPOL standards for international cybercrime cooperation and evidence exchange.Section 5: International Comparative Legal and Tactical Gap AnalysisComparing the Indian statutory framework (BNS, BSA, BNSS, IT Act, and 1. Judicial Rejection of EvidenceUnder Sections 61 and 63 of the BSA, the submission of a secondary electronic record without an unbroken chain-of-custody log or a valid Section 63(4) certificate is a fatal procedural defect. In Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v. Kailash Kushanrao Gorantyal, the Supreme Court of India affirmed that the electronic evidence certificate is non-negotiable and must accompany the digital record at each instance of its production.Similarly, in State of Rajasthan v. Tara Singh, the Supreme Court held that the absence or improper maintenance of Malkhana registers, or failure to prove that the seals remained intact from the point of seizure to laboratory testing, casts reasonable doubt on the prosecution's case, resulting in the absolute exclusion of the evidence and the acquittal of the accused.2. Financial Penalties under the DPDP Act, 2023While law enforcement agencies are exempt from data subject consent requirements during active investigations, they are explicitly bound by Section 8(5) to maintain reasonable security safeguards to prevent personal data breaches. If the Digital Malkhana suffers a data breach due to security negligence (such as weak access governance, lack of encryption, or failure to implement OWASP ASVS Level 3 controls), the Data Protection Board of India (DPBI) can impose massive financial penalties under Section 15 of the Act :Failure to take reasonable security safeguards to prevent data breach: Penalties up to Rs 250 crore.Failure to notify the Board and affected data principals of a personal data breach: Penalties up to Rs 150 crore.3.
